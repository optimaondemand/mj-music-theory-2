Harmonic Analysis: The Complete Method
Quarter 2 · Module 15
Today's Focus

Harmonic analysis is how musicians read a chord progression the way a reader reads a sentence – not just word by word, but understanding how each part contributes to the whole meaning. In this lesson you will learn a six-step method for labeling every chord in a passage, identifying what role each one plays, and writing a short paragraph connecting those harmonic choices to the music's expressive effect.

Objectives
  • 1
    Perform a complete Roman numeral analysis of an 8–16 measure passage in major or minor, including inversions, seventh chords, secondary dominants, and borrowed chords.
  • 2
    Identify all secondary dominants, borrowed chords, and cadence types in an analyzed passage.
  • 3
    Write a harmonic analysis paragraph that connects a passage's chord progression to its expressive function.
Materials
  • Open Music Theory (OMT), pages 411, 412, and 530
  • Staff paper or your notebook

Part 1: The Six-Step Method

OMT (p. 412) lays out a reliable sequence for analyzing any tonal passage. Working through steps in order – rather than jumping to what looks interesting – prevents the most common errors, like mislabeling a secondary dominant as a simple triad or missing a borrowed chord because you assumed the key was pure major.

1
Determine the Key
Read the key signature. Confirm the tonal center by noting which pitch phrases begin and end on. Decide major or minor. Flag any accidentals that might suggest a modal shift later.
2
Identify Root and Quality
For each chord, name the bass note and stack the intervals above it. Determine whether the triad is major, minor, augmented, or diminished – and whether a 7th is present.
3
Assign Roman Numerals and Inversion Figures
Match the root to its scale degree. Use uppercase for major quality, lowercase for minor, the degree symbol for diminished. Add inversion figures (6, 6/4, 7, 6/5, 4/3, 2) if the chord is not in root position.
4
Find Secondary Dominants
Look for chromatic pitches – notes outside the established key. A raised chromatic pitch often signals a secondary dominant: a V or V7 applied to a chord other than I. Label it V/IV, V7/V, and so on.
5
Find Borrowed Chords
A chord that uses a scale degree from the parallel key is a borrowed chord (modal mixture). In major, watch for iv, ♭VI, and ♭VII as common borrowed sonorities. Label them with the flat sign if needed.
6
Mark Cadences
At each phrase ending, label the cadence type: PAC (perfect authentic – V-I root position, tonic in soprano), IAC (imperfect authentic), HC (half cadence on V), or DC (deceptive – V-vi).

Notice that secondary dominants (step 4) come after you have assigned basic Roman numerals (step 3). This matters: you need the key established and the chord quality confirmed before you can recognize that a chromatic pitch is producing a tonicization.

Part 2: The Phrase Function Model

OMT (p. 411) describes the T–PD–D–T phrase model, which names the harmonic function of each part of a phrase rather than just its Roman numeral. A tonic chord can appear at the beginning or the end; its function depends on context. Labeling function helps you describe what the passage is doing, not just which chords appear.

T → PD → D → T: one complete phrase arc. Each section can contain multiple chords; this shows the most common single-chord representatives.

A tonic prolongation at the start (T) gives the phrase its grounding. The predominant zone (PD) introduces motion away from tonic without yet creating the full tension of the dominant. The dominant zone (D) is the point of highest harmonic tension – it drives toward resolution. The final tonic (T) closes the phrase and, at a PAC, confirms the key decisively.

When you annotate a score, you can write T, PD, D in brackets above or below the staff in addition to the Roman numerals. This two-layer labeling (function + numeral) is the complete analytical annotation described in OMT.

Part 3: Harmonic Rhythm

Harmonic rhythm is the rate at which chords change. A passage where one chord spans two or three measures feels settled and spacious – the harmony is not pressing forward. A passage where chords shift every beat or half-beat feels urgent and driven. Noticing harmonic rhythm is part of a complete analysis because it maps directly onto the emotional shape of the music.

One chord lasts two or more measures. The music breathes; tension is low.

In your analysis paragraph, mentioning harmonic rhythm turns a list of Roman numerals into an argument about expressive effect. Instead of "the passage uses I, IV, ii, V, I," you can write: "the harmonic rhythm accelerates into the cadence, with chords changing every beat in the final measure – a technique that heightens the sense of arrival at the PAC."

Think About It

When you label V7/ii in a passage, what are you telling the reader about the music? What does that label explain about the harmonic motion at that moment?

Write two or three sentences in your notebook before moving to the Canvas discussion. The goal is to put the idea into your own words first, then refine it when you see what classmates say.

🤖 WORTH KNOWING · HARMONIC ANALYSIS AND AI
AI chord identification apps exist and can move fast through a lead sheet or score, but they frequently mislabel ambiguous or chromatic passages, especially secondary dominants and borrowed chords. The six step method in this lesson is exactly what catches those mistakes, because it forces you to establish the key and confirm each chord's quality before you go looking for anything chromatic.

The One Thing to Remember

Work through the six steps in order every time – key first, root and quality second, Roman numerals third. Chromatic pitches and borrowed chords only become visible once you have the key established and the basic label assigned. The method is a filter: each step removes ambiguity before you face the next one.
