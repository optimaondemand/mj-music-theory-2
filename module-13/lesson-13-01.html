LESSON 13.01
Species Counterpoint: All Five Species
🎓 TODAY'S FOCUS
How does adding one rule at a time – from a single consonant note to tied dissonances to completely free mixing – build a composer toward writing truly expressive two-voice music?
🎯 OBJECTIVES
1
Write a correct first-, second-, and third-species counterpoint to a given cantus firmus.
2
Explain the function of each species in terms of how it treats dissonance.
3
Identify the characteristic treatment of dissonance in fourth-species counterpoint, including the preparation, suspension, and resolution.
📚 MATERIALS
  • Open Music Theory (OMT), pp. 202–233
  • Notebook and staff paper, or MuseScore (or another notation app)
  • Headphones
🎶 PART 1 · FIRST SPECIES: NOTE AGAINST NOTE

First species is the strictest form: you write exactly one note above (or below) each note of the cantus firmus. Every single note must form a consonant interval with the cantus firmus. No exceptions.

The permitted consonances are: perfect unison (P1), major and minor thirds (3rd), perfect fifth (P5), major and minor sixths (6th), and perfect octave (P8). All other intervals – seconds, fourths, sevenths – are dissonant and not allowed in first species.

Additional rules: begin and end on P1, P5, or P8. Avoid parallel perfect fifths and parallel perfect octaves between any two adjacent notes.

KEY POINT
The most important motion type in counterpoint is contrary motion: when the cantus firmus moves up, your counterpoint moves down, and vice versa. This independence between voices is what counterpoint is all about. Contrary motion prevents parallel fifths and octaves naturally and creates a sense of two genuine voices rather than one melody with a shadow.
🎶 PART 2 · THE FIVE SPECIES

Each species adds one new technique to your toolkit. Think of them as five steps on a ladder, moving from the strictest rules to the most expressive freedom.

Second species gives you two half notes against each whole note of the cantus firmus. The note on beat 1 must be consonant. The note on beat 2 may be a passing tone – a dissonance that steps smoothly between two consonances.

Third species increases motion to four quarter notes per cantus firmus note. In addition to passing tones, you may now use neighbor tones: a note that steps away from a consonance and immediately steps back to it. This creates melodic decoration without losing harmonic stability.

Fourth species introduces the most expressive device: the suspension. You tie a note over the barline into a strong beat where it creates a dissonance, then resolve it down by step. We will cover this in detail in Part 3.

Fifth species (florid counterpoint) combines all four previous species freely. Long notes mark structural moments; shorter note values carry motion and expression. Fifth species is the closest counterpoint comes to actual free composition, and it is the foundation of two-voice writing in the Baroque period.

🎶 PART 3 · FOURTH SPECIES: SUSPENSIONS

The suspension is the most expressive device in species counterpoint. Instead of resolving a dissonance immediately, you hold a consonant note across the barline into a strong beat where it becomes dissonant – and then, and only then, you resolve it. That delay is what gives suspensions their emotional power.

Every suspension has three stages:

  1. Preparation: The note sounds on a weak beat as a consonance. There is no surprise yet.
  2. Suspension: That same note is held (or tied) into the following strong beat. At that moment, because the cantus firmus has moved, it forms a dissonance. This clash is the emotional tension.
  3. Resolution: The suspended note steps down by one step to a consonance. The tension releases. This step downward is not optional – it is a strict rule.

Suspensions are labeled with two numbers counting intervals above the bass: the interval of the suspension followed by the interval of the resolution.

Label Suspension Interval Resolves To Notes
4-3 4th above bass (dissonant) 3rd above bass Most common
7-6 7th above bass (dissonant) 6th above bass Very common in two voices
9-8 9th above bass (dissonant) 8th above bass (octave) Creates strong arrival
2-1 2nd above bass (dissonant) Unison Less common; use carefully
KEY POINT
Suspensions resolve DOWN by step, always. Moving up by step, or leaping in any direction, breaks the rule. The downward resolution is what gives the suspension its sense of release and arrival.

🌟 THE ONE THING TO REMEMBER
Suspensions are the heart of fourth species: a consonance is prepared on a weak beat, held across the barline so it becomes a dissonance on the strong beat, and then resolved down by step to a consonance. That three-part sequence – preparation, suspension, resolution – is the pattern to carry with you into all your counterpoint writing.
