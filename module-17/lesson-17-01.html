🎵 TODAY'S FOCUS
Jazz combines African rhythmic traditions and European harmonic practice. How does that combination create something neither tradition produced alone?
Jazz did not emerge from a single tradition. It grew at the crossroads of African rhythmic practices (swing feel, call-and-response, improvisation) and European harmonic conventions (chord progressions, voice leading, functional harmony). The result was a new music with its own logic, vocabulary, and expressive range. Today you trace that history and begin reading the symbols jazz performers use.

🎶 PART 1 · JAZZ HISTORY

Jazz emerged in New Orleans around 1900 as African American musicians blended blues, ragtime, marching band music, and the improvisational spirit of African musical traditions. Over the next century it evolved through a series of distinct styles, each building on and reacting to what came before.

New Orleans Jazz (ca. 1900–1920) featured collective improvisation among a small front-line group (cornet, clarinet, trombone) over a rhythm section. Blues and march traditions both contributed. Louis Armstrong was the defining soloist of this era and the transition into the next.

Swing (1930s) scaled jazz up to large ensembles called big bands, with sections of brass, reeds, and rhythm players. Written arrangements guided the ensemble while soloists improvised. Jazz became American popular dance music. Duke Ellington, Count Basie, and Benny Goodman led major bands.

Bebop (1940s) was a reaction against commercial dance music. Young musicians including Charlie Parker and Dizzy Gillespie developed a complex, fast-paced style for listening rather than dancing. Bebop features dense chromatic harmony, rapid tempos, and virtuosic improvisation over intricate chord progressions.

Cool Jazz (1950s) dialed the intensity back, favoring smoother tone, more relaxed tempos, and a lighter touch. Miles Davis's recording Birth of the Cool (1957) is a landmark. Hard Bop (1950s–60s) reintegrated blues and gospel influences into the bebop framework. Modal Jazz (1960s) replaced dense chord changes with modes as the basis for improvisation – Miles Davis's Kind of Blue (1959) is the defining recording. Free Jazz (1960s–70s) abandoned fixed meter and chord progressions entirely. Fusion (1970s) merged jazz improvisation with rock rhythms and electric instruments. Contemporary jazz continues to draw from all of these traditions.

🎸 PART 2 · SWING RHYTHM

Swing is the rhythmic foundation of jazz. When a performer plays two consecutive eighth notes, the first note is held longer than the second – roughly in a 2:1 ratio. This long-short pattern, called swing feel, replaces the even, mechanical division of a beat into two equal halves.

Straight (even):

50%
50%
Both notes equal duration. Counted: "one and two and…"
Swung (long-short, approximately 2:1):

≈67% (long)

Ratio: approximately 2:1 (long–short)
In notation, swing is often written as straight eighth notes with the instruction "Swing" at the top of the chart. The performer knows to apply the long-short feel.

The 2:1 ratio is a guideline, not a rigid rule. At slower tempos the swing is more pronounced (closer to 3:1, resembling a triplet feel). At very fast bebop tempos it approaches straight eighths. Experienced jazz performers adjust instinctively to the tempo and the other musicians.

💡 TRY IT IN YOUR NOTEBOOK
Before reading on, tap or clap a steady beat and say "du-da du-da" over each beat, making the "du" about twice as long as the "da." That is swing feel. Write one sentence in your notebook describing what changed from straight counting.
🎹 PART 3 · JAZZ CHORD SYMBOLS

Jazz performers read from lead sheets, which show only the melody and chord symbols. The chord symbols tell you what harmony to play but leave the voicing, timing, and texture up to you. Learning to decode them is essential for any jazz musician.

Jazz Chord Symbol Decoder
Cmaj7
C major 7th
Major triad + major 7th
Notes: C – E – G – B
B (maj 7th) G (5th) E (3rd) C (root)
Dm7
D minor 7th
Minor triad + minor 7th
Notes: D – F – A – C
C (min 7th) A (5th) F (min 3rd) D (root)
G7
G dominant 7th
Major triad + minor 7th
Notes: G – B – D – F
F (min 7th) D (5th) B (maj 3rd) G (root)
Plain "7" = lowered (minor) 7th. No "maj" = not major 7th.
Cm7♭5
C half-diminished 7th
Diminished triad + minor 7th
Notes: C – E♭ – G♭ – B♭
B♭ (min 7th) G♭ (dim 5th) E♭ (min 3rd) C (root)
Also written Cø7. The ♭5 lowers the fifth to make a diminished triad.
Key rule: "maj7" = major 7th  |  plain "7" = minor 7th  |  "♭5" = lowered fifth

In practice, the four chord types above appear constantly in jazz progressions. The ii–V–I progression (Dm7 – G7 – Cmaj7 in C major) is the most common harmonic formula in jazz, equivalent to the ii–V–I in classical harmony. You will use these symbols throughout the rest of the course.

💡 KEY POINT
A jazz standard is a song from the Great American Songbook (or jazz repertoire) that has been performed widely across generations. Standards such as Autumn Leaves, All the Things You Are, and Misty all use the chord types you just learned. Knowing these symbols lets you read a lead sheet for any standard.

💪 THE ONE THING TO REMEMBER

Jazz grew from the convergence of African rhythmic tradition and European harmonic practice. Its defining rhythmic feel is swing (long-short, approximately 2:1). Its performers read lead sheets with chord symbols (Cmaj7, Dm7, G7, Cm7♭5) that name the harmony while leaving voicing and timing to the musician. Every jazz standard you encounter will use these tools.
