LESSON 9.01
Borrowing from the Shadows: Modal Mixture
Lesson
🎵 TODAY'S FOCUS
A piece is clearly in a major key -- then one chord arrives and everything darkens. The major key is still home, but something else just walked in.
That darkening chord is almost certainly a borrowed chord from the parallel minor. This technique is called modal mixture. The composer keeps the major key as home but imports one or more chords from the parallel minor for color and emotional depth. It is one of the most powerful tools in tonal harmony, and you hear it in pop, rock, gospel, hymns, and film scores every day.

🎵 PART 1 · WHAT IS MODAL MIXTURE?

Modal mixture means borrowing chords from the parallel minor (or major) while the piece stays in the other mode. When a piece is in C major and the composer uses a chord from C minor, that chord is borrowed. The tonic stays on C. Only the quality of one or more chords changes.

The tell is ♭6 -- the flatted sixth scale degree. Every borrowed chord from the parallel minor introduces ♭6 or another lowered pitch. In C major, ♭6 = A♭. When you see or hear A♭ in a C major passage, a borrowed chord is nearby.

Each chord comes from C natural minor (C D E♭ F G A♭ B♭). The ♭6 (A♭) appears in all but ♭VII, making it the most reliable "borrowed chord" signal.
🎹 PART 2 · THE MINOR iv -- A DARKENING PREDOMINANT

The most emotionally powerful mixture chord is iv -- the minor subdominant. In C major, that is F minor (F–A♭–C). It typically appears in the predominant position, just before a cadential chord (V or the cadential 6/4), creating a darkening effect right before the resolution.

The ♭6 (A♭) in the iv chord should resolve downward by step to G (scale degree 5) when the progression moves to V. This voice-leading rule keeps the borrowed chord from sounding jarring.
💡 VOICE-LEADING RULE
The ♭6 in any borrowed chord resolves downward by step to scale degree 5. In C major: A♭ → G. Never use ♭6 in a way that forces it to resolve upward -- that creates an awkward augmented 2nd or an uncharacteristic sound.
🎸 PART 3 · YOU HAVE HEARD THIS BEFORE

Modal mixture is everywhere in the music you already know. The ♭VI and ♭VII borrowed chords are especially common in rock and pop because they add Mixolydian and minor-mode color without leaving major:

♭VI in rock / pop / film
In C major: A♭ major (♭VI). Common chord in anthemic rock, power ballads, and epic film scores. The abrupt shift to this chord creates a sense of grandeur or heartbreak. Think of the big "lift" moment in many pop songs.
♭VII in rock / folk
In C major: B♭ major (♭VII). This chord comes from Mixolydian coloring as well as borrowed from minor. It appears in classic rock, folk-rock, and country -- a "cool" lowered chord that still feels major and energetic.
iv in pop / gospel / hymns
In C major: F minor (iv). Creates a deeply emotional, often bittersweet moment. Very common in gospel and contemporary Christian music, pop bridges, and film scoring at emotional climaxes.
♭VI → ♭VII → I (the "epic" cadence)
Borrowed ♭VI to ♭VII to I is one of the most recognizable cadential formulas in rock and film. It creates a dramatic, powerful buildup to the tonic. You have heard it in countless movie trailers and rock anthems.

💪 THE ONE THING TO REMEMBER

Modal mixture keeps the same tonic but imports a chord from the parallel mode. The ♭6 is the tell -- when you see or hear that lowered sixth scale degree, a borrowed chord is at work. The minor iv before a cadence is the most emotionally potent version. ♭VI and ♭VII are rock and pop staples. Find ♭6, and you have found the mixture.
