Natural Minor and Its Harmonies

🎵 TODAY'S FOCUS
Why does minor music sound the way it does, and what makes it different from major?
You already know major scales and their harmonies. Natural minor is built from the same notes, just starting on a different pitch, yet the whole emotional landscape shifts. Today you map that new territory: the step pattern, the diatonic chords, and the one harmonic quirk that set composers searching for something more.

🎶 PART 1 · THE NATURAL MINOR SCALE

Natural minor is built on the pattern W-H-W-W-H-W-W. Two half steps fall in specific places: between scale degrees 2-3 and between 5-6. Everything else is a whole step. The simplest natural minor scale uses no sharps or flats: A natural minor (A-B-C-D-E-F-G-A).

Red HS = half step. Half steps fall at 2-3 and 5-6. The top A is omitted from the right-side step labels because no step follows the octave.

Compare that to major (W-W-H-W-W-W-H): natural minor pushes both half steps earlier and changes where they land. The result is scale degrees 3, 6, and 7 are each a half step lower than in major. Those three flatted degrees are the engine of the minor sound.

C Major vs. A Natural Minor: Scale Degree Comparison
💡 KEY POINT
Natural minor is also known as the Aeolian mode. It uses the key signature without any alteration. A minor uses no sharps or flats; E minor uses one sharp (F♯); D minor uses one flat (B♭).
🎸 PART 2 · DIATONIC TRIADS IN NATURAL MINOR

Stack a third and a fifth on each scale degree. The seven triads you get are called the diatonic triads of natural minor. Roman numerals tell you the root; lowercase = minor, UPPERCASE = major, and the degree sign ° = diminished.

Diatonic Triads of A Natural Minor

Notice the red v chord. In major, the V chord is major, and it contains a leading tone: scale degree 7 is a half step below the tonic, creating a strong pull home. In natural minor, scale degree 7 is a whole step below the tonic. That note is called the subtonic, and the v chord built on it is minor. A minor v chord lacks the leading-tone tension. It does not pull strongly back to i.

⚠ THE HARMONIC PROBLEM
In natural minor: v-i sounds weak. Both chords are minor, scale degree 7 steps down by a whole step instead of pulling up by a half step, and the cadence lacks the resolution power of V-i in major. This is precisely why composers developed harmonic minor (coming up in Module 3).

The VII chord (the subtonic triad: G major in A minor) does sound satisfying in natural minor, but in a different way. It resolves by stepping downward (G to A) rather than by the half-step pull of a leading tone. You hear this sound constantly in rock, folk, and modal music.

💪 THE ONE THING TO REMEMBER

Natural minor (W-H-W-W-H-W-W) lowers scale degrees 3, 6, and 7. Its seven diatonic triads include a minor v chord (no leading tone, weak dominant) and a major VII chord (the subtonic). The minor v is the harmonic problem that drives the development of harmonic minor. Keep that problem in mind as you move to Module 3.
