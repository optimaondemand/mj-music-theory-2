🎵 TODAY'S FOCUS
What happens when you add one more note on top of a triad, and why does that extra note create so much harmonic energy?
You know triads well. A seventh chord stacks one more third on top, giving you four notes and a new, richer sound. Today you will identify all five seventh-chord qualities, map every diatonic seventh chord in C major, and learn how the ii7 chord acts as a powerful predominant that smoothly drives toward V.

🎶 PART 1 · THE FIVE SEVENTH-CHORD QUALITIES

A seventh chord adds one more third above a triad, producing a four-note stack: root, third, fifth, and seventh. The combination of triad quality and the size of that top third creates five distinct flavors. Each has a name, a symbol, and an interval recipe.

The Five Seventh-Chord Qualities = built on C
Each stack reads bottom-to-top: root → 3rd → 5th → 7th. The interval recipe (bottom tag) lists the three stacked thirds.
💡 QUICK MEMORY AID
The dominant seventh (V7) is the most important: it contains the tritone (M3 + m3 + m3) and has the strongest pull to the tonic. The fully diminished seventh is made entirely of minor thirds; that symmetry means it can resolve in multiple directions, making it one of the most ambiguous chords in tonal music.

In your notebook, write the five names and their recipes. Then try building each one from the note D. Compare your answers to the chart above when you are done.

🎸 PART 2 · DIATONIC SEVENTH CHORDS IN C MAJOR

Stack a third, fifth, and seventh on every note of the C major scale using only white-key notes. You get seven diatonic seventh chords, each with its own quality. The pattern of qualities is fixed for every major key.

Diatonic Seventh Chords of C Major

Notice the pattern: Imaj7, IVmaj7 (major-seventh quality), ii7, iii7, vi7 (minor-seventh quality), V7 (dominant-seventh quality), viiø7 (half-diminished quality). This exact pattern repeats in every major key. If you know the key, you know the quality of every seventh chord automatically.

🎙 PART 3 · THE PREDOMINANT SEVENTH AND VOICE LEADING

The ii7 chord (Dm7 in C major) is the most important predominant seventh. It sits in the ii area of the harmonic hierarchy (tonic → predominant → dominant → tonic), and its seventh creates a smooth stepwise connection to V7. The 7th of the ii7 chord must resolve downward by step.

ii7 – V7 – I: The Predominant Seventh in Action (C major)
💡 VOICE-LEADING RULE
The 7th of any seventh chord resolves downward by step. In the ii7-V7-I progression (Dm7 → G7 → Cmaj7), the C in Dm7 moves down to B in G7. Never double the 7th. Never resolve the 7th upward.

In minor keys, the predominant seventh is the half-diminished iiø7. In A minor, that chord is B-D-F-A. It moves to E7 (the major V7 built on the raised leading tone from harmonic minor), then resolves to Am. The voice-leading rule is identical: the 7th (A in the iiø7) resolves down a step to G♯ in E7.

💪 THE ONE THING TO REMEMBER

Five seventh-chord qualities; the dominant seventh (V7) has the strongest pull. In any major key, the ii7 is a minor seventh chord that acts as the most powerful predominant: its 7th resolves down by step into V7, which then resolves to I. That three-chord pattern (ii7 → V7 → I) is one of the most important progressions in tonal music.
