LESSON 18.01

Jazz Harmony: Extensions and Substitutions

Today’s Focus

The ii–V–I progression appears in virtually every jazz standard. Why is this three-chord pattern the “DNA of jazz”?

  • 1Add extensions (9ths, 11ths, 13ths) to basic jazz chord types.
  • 2Apply the tritone substitution to a dominant 7th chord.
  • 3Identify the chord-scale relationship for ii–V–I in major.
Materials: Open Music Theory (OMT) pp. 545–562 · Music notebook or MuseScore
Part 1

Chord Extensions

A basic 7th chord stacks thirds up to the 7th above the root: root, 3rd, 5th, 7th. Jazz composers and improvisers keep stacking: they add the 9th, 11th, and 13th. These pitches are called extensions. They add color and tension to a chord without changing its fundamental function.

The 9th is a major 2nd above the octave (one octave + a 2nd from the root). The 11th is a perfect 4th above the octave. The 13th is a major 6th above the octave. When a chord symbol says Cmaj9, it means Cmaj7 with the 9th (D) added. G13 implies G7 with the 9th, 11th, and 13th all available; performers select which to voice in any given moment.

Extensions add color above the 7th. In practice, performers choose which to include.

A special case is the altered dominant: a dominant 7th chord whose 9th and/or 5th are chromatically raised or lowered (♭9, ♯9, ♭5/♯11, ♭13). These alterations create maximum dissonance that resolves with extra force to the tonic chord.

Part 2

Tritone Substitution

In a ii–V–I in C major, the V7 chord is G7. Jazz musicians discovered that G7 can be replaced by a chord whose root is a tritone (three whole steps, or 6 half steps) away: D♭7. This is the tritone substitution.

The reason it works is mathematical. Both G7 and D♭7 contain the same two “guide tones” (the pitches B and F or their enharmonic equivalents). Those two notes form a tritone, and tritones are what give dominant chords their pull toward resolution. When the guide tones are the same, the listener’s ear hears the same tension, no matter which chord is underneath.

D♭7 substitutes for G7 because both contain the critical tritone B–F. The D♭ bass moves by half step to C instead of the usual fourth.

The chromatic bass motion (D♭ → C) is one of the smoothest possible voice leadings, and it gives the substitution a distinctive sound heard throughout jazz recordings.

Part 3

Chord-Scale Theory and the ii–V–I

Chord-scale theory pairs each chord in a progression with a specific scale. The scale does not change the chord; it gives the improviser a “roadmap” of which notes will sound best over that chord. In the ii–V–I, three different modes fit the three chords, even though all three belong to the same key.

Chord Function Scale to Use
Dm7 (ii7) Predominant D Dorian
G7 (V7) Dominant G Mixolydian (or G Altered for color)
Cmaj7 (Imaj7) Tonic C Lydian or C Ionian

The ii–V–I progression is the foundation of jazz harmony. Each chord has a matching scale that guides improvisation.

Notice that D Dorian, G Mixolydian, and C Ionian all use the same seven pitches (the C major scale), just starting from different roots. Over G7, an improviser might also choose the G altered scale (which has lowered and raised extensions) for a more dissonant, tension-filled sound that pushes powerfully into the Cmaj7 resolution.

🤖 WORTH KNOWING · JAZZ HARMONY AND AI
AI tools can suggest a jazzy reharmonization or generate a tritone substitution on request almost instantly. What they typically do not explain is why a tritone substitution works, that D flat 7 shares the same guide tones as G7, which is the reasoning this lesson actually builds. Understanding that shared tritone, not just producing the substitution, is the skill being assessed.

One Thing to Remember

The ii–V–I is built on guide tones: the 3rd and 7th of each chord. Those two notes carry the harmonic function of the progression. The tritone substitution works because it preserves those guide tones while changing the bass. Everything else in jazz harmony (extensions, altered dominants, chord-scale choices) builds on that foundation.
