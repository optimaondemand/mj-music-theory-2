Score Analysis: Public Domain Works

Quarter 2 · Module 16 · Form and Counterpoint

🎮 Today's Focus

Every skill you have built in Theory II comes together here. You have analyzed keys, labeled Roman numerals, identified form, named secondary dominants, and described cadences. In this lesson you apply all of it to a real score – a piece composed centuries ago, available free of charge, and ready for you to study in detail.

The lesson is intentionally compact. The main work is the score analysis essay you will write after finishing this page. Read carefully, use the checklist and essay structure below, then open IMSLP and get started.

🎯 Objectives

1
Analyze a public domain piano or vocal piece for key, form, and harmonic language.
2
Identify at least two techniques studied in Theory II within your chosen piece.
3
Write a structured analytical essay connecting formal structure to harmonic content.

📚 Materials

  • Open Music Theory (OMT) – all analysis chapters as reference
  • IMSLP (imslp.org) – free public domain scores; no account required for most downloads
  • Word or Google Docs – for drafting and submitting your essay
  • Your notebook – for sketching Roman numerals and form diagrams before you write

Part 1: What Is Public Domain?

Before you can analyze a score, you need to find one you are legally free to use. That is where public domain and IMSLP come in.

🌎 Public Domain & IMSLP

Public domain means the copyright on a work has expired. For music, this generally applies to works by composers who died before approximately 1930. You can download, print, study, and reproduce these scores without permission or payment.

IMSLP (the International Music Score Library Project) is the primary free repository of public domain music scores. It hosts hundreds of thousands of scores in PDF format. Most scores are available immediately without creating an account.

→ Visit IMSLP at imslp.org to browse by composer, instrument, or genre.

Good starting points for this essay: J.S. Bach (Well-Tempered Clavier, chorales), Beethoven (early piano sonatas, Op. 2–22), Schubert (lieder), Schumann (Album for the Young, Op. 68).

Part 2: The Analysis Checklist

When you open a score for the first time, work through these six steps in order. Each step gives you information that makes the next step easier.

✍ Score Analysis Checklist

1
Key / Mode
Identify the key signature. Confirm major or minor (or modal) from context. Write the tonic.
2
Form + Sections
Mark where sections begin and end. Label them A, B, A′, etc. Note any repeats.
3
Harmony (RN / secondary dominants / mixture / 7ths)
Add Roman numerals measure by measure. Flag secondary dominants (V/x), borrowed chords, and seventh chords.
4
Cadences
Identify the cadence type at the end of each phrase: authentic, half, deceptive, or plagal.
5
Texture
Describe the texture (monophonic, homophonic, polyphonic). Note any changes and where they occur.
6
Modal / Chromatic Features
Note any accidentals that do not belong to the key. Are they chromatic passing tones, borrowed chords, or tonicizations?

Work through the checklist in your notebook before you begin writing. Your essay will draw directly from what you find here.

For Step 5, do not just restate what you found. Connect your harmonic observations to something expressive: why does the deceptive cadence feel unfinished? What does the secondary dominant do to the forward energy of the phrase? That connection is the conclusion's job.

▶ What You'll Do Next

  2. Complete the score analysis essay. Work through the checklist in your notebook, then write your 400–600 word structured essay in Word or Google Docs. Attach or embed the relevant score pages.
  3. Peer discussion. In the module discussion, share one chord from your analysis and explain what it does. Read a classmate's post and respond with one observation they might have missed.

✎ The One Thing to Remember

Identifying a chord is the beginning, not the analysis. The essay asks you to move one step further: once you have labeled the chord, explain what it does. What does it create? What does it delay? What changes because of it? That sentence – from label to meaning – is what makes an analysis an analysis.
