🎵 TODAY'S FOCUS
How do composers control when a phrase feels finished, partially resolved, or deliberately avoided?
You already know the four basic cadence types. Today you extend that vocabulary with three more: the cadential 6/4 (which strengthens any authentic cadence), the Phrygian half cadence (a distinctive minor-key sound), and the evaded cadence (which refuses to arrive). Along the way you build the complete cadence gallery you will need for analysis and composition.

3
I will be able to identify all seven cadence types from a chord progression and label each correctly.

🎶 PART 1 · THE CADENTIAL 6/4

The cadential 6/4 (labeled Cad. 6/4 or I⁶₄) is a tonic chord in second inversion placed directly before V. It uses the notes of the tonic triad, but it does not function as a tonic chord. It belongs to the dominant function, acting as a decoration of V. The 6th and 4th above the bass each resolve downward by step over the same bass note.

The Cadential 6/4 in C major: I⁶₄ – V – I
⚠ THE KEY INSIGHT
The cadential 6/4 looks like a tonic chord (it uses C, E, G in C major) but it functions as part of V. The bass note stays on scale degree 5 (G), and the 6th and 4th above it resolve downward. Think of it as an ornament on V, not as a stable I. When you analyze it, label it Cad. 6/4 or V⁶₄, not as I.
🎹 PART 2 · THE PHRYGIAN HALF CADENCE

The Phrygian half cadence is a minor-mode cadence where the phrase ends on V, approached by iv6 (the minor subdominant in first inversion). Its signature is the stepwise descending bass: scale degree ⒵ steps down by a half step to scale degree ⒴ (5). That half-step drop in the bass is what gives it the Phrygian sound. Bach used it constantly in slow movements and inner phrase endings.

Phrygian Half Cadence: iv6 – V (in A minor)

Three things to remember about the Phrygian half cadence: (1) it only occurs in minor keys; (2) the bass descends by a half step into V (scale degree 6 to 5, a semitone in minor); (3) despite landing on V, it is still a half cadence because the phrase ends without reaching i. Bach used it at the end of inner phrases and at fermatas in slow movements.

💡 WHY THE NAME?
The Phrygian mode has a half step from scale degree 1 up to 2, giving it a distinctive descending minor-second motion at the bottom. The iv6-V cadence in minor creates that same descending half step in the bass (⒵ to ⒴), evoking the modal color even in a tonal context.
🎺 PART 3 · THE COMPLETE CADENCE GALLERY

You now have the full vocabulary. Study the gallery below, then use the flip cards to test yourself. The chord motion column shows the essential harmonic motion; the key detail is what that motion means for the sense of arrival or departure.

Cadence Gallery = Complete Reference
Name Chord Motion Sense of Arrival Key Detail
Perfect Authentic (PAC) V (root pos.) → I (root pos.) Full, strong Both chords in root position; melody ends on scale degree 1. Strongest possible ending.
Imperfect Authentic (IAC) V → I (inversion or melody on 3/5) Partial Authentic motion but melody does not end on 1, or one chord is inverted. Still resolves to I but feels less final.
Half Cadence (HC) Any chord → V Open, question Phrase ends on V. Feels unfinished; listener expects continuation. Can be approached from I, ii, IV, or iv.
Phrygian Half Cadence iv6 → V (minor only) Open, dark color Bass descends by half step (scale deg. ⒵ to ⒴). Only in minor keys. Characteristic of Baroque music; Bach uses it constantly.
Plagal (PC) IV → I (or iv → i in minor) Soft, settled The "amen" cadence. Softer than authentic; no leading tone. iv-i in minor sounds modal and archaic.
Deceptive (DC) V → vi (major) or V → VI (minor) Surprise V seems about to resolve to I but moves to vi instead. Extends the phrase by denying expected resolution.
Evaded Cadence V → unexpected harmony (often I⁶ or V⁷) Denied The dominant sets up a cadence expectation but sidesteps it entirely. The phrase is forced to continue; creates momentum or tension.

Notice that the PAC, IAC, and deceptive cadences all begin with V. What separates them is where V goes. The half cadence and Phrygian half cadence both end on V. The plagal cadence is the only one that never touches V at all.

💪 THE ONE THING TO REMEMBER

The cadential 6/4 looks like a tonic chord but functions as dominant because the 6th resolves down to 5 and the 4th resolves down to 3 over the same bass note. The Phrygian half cadence (iv6 – V) is identified by its stepwise descending bass from scale degree ⒵ to ⒴ in minor. Together with PAC, IAC, half, plagal, deceptive, and evaded cadences, you now have the complete vocabulary for analyzing how any phrase in tonal music ends.
