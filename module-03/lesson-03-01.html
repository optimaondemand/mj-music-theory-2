LESSON 3.01
Harmonic Minor and the Augmented Triad
Lesson
🎵 TODAY'S FOCUS
One raised note solves the harmonic problem from Module 2. What else does it create along the way?
In Module 2 you found that natural minor's v chord is weak: no leading tone, no strong pull home. Composers solved that by raising scale degree 7 by one half step. That single change creates harmonic minor, restores the strong V-i cadence, and produces two fascinating side effects: an exotic sound called the augmented 2nd and a new chord type called the augmented triad.

🎶 PART 1 · THE HARMONIC MINOR SCALE

Start with natural minor. Raise scale degree 7 by one half step. That is the only change. The raised 7th is always written as an accidental in the score, not in the key signature, because it is a modification of the key, not a permanent part of it.

In A harmonic minor: the scale runs A-B-C-D-E-F-G♯-A. The G is raised to G♯. G♯ is now one half step below A, so it pulls strongly upward to the tonic. That is the definition of a leading tone.

A Harmonic Minor: Raised Scale Degree 7
💡 THE AUGMENTED 2ND
Between scale degrees 6 and 7 in harmonic minor there is a gap of 3 half steps. A normal major 2nd is 2 half steps; this one is stretched (augmented) by one. You cannot sing it as a smooth step. It leaps, and that leap is the distinctive exotic quality you hear in harmonic minor pieces. It is the trade-off composers accepted to get the strong leading tone they needed.
🎹 PART 2 · THE AUGMENTED TRIAD ON III

When you raise scale degree 7, you change what triad sits on scale degree 3. In natural minor, III is a major triad (C-E-G in A minor). In harmonic minor, the G is raised to G♯. Now the triad on III is C-E-G♯: a root, major third, and augmented fifth. That is an augmented triad, written III+ or III+.

The Augmented Triad on III (III+) in A Harmonic Minor: C-E-G♯
Two stacked major thirds produce an augmented fifth (8 half steps) instead of a perfect fifth (7 half steps). That extra half step is what makes the triad augmented.

An augmented triad sounds tense and unstable because the top note (the augmented fifth) does not settle the chord. It wants to resolve. Composers used it as a color chord, not as a home base. You will often see III+ resolving to the iv chord in harmonic minor pieces.

📚 OMT NOTE
OMT p. 183 confirms: the augmented triad on III arises in harmonic minor when scale degrees 3-5-7 are used and degree 7 is raised. The symbol III+ means augmented quality on scale degree 3. The + (plus sign) is used because + = augmented in chord symbols.

✅ THE PAYOFF
The V chord in harmonic minor is now major (E-G♯-B in A harmonic minor). It contains G♯, the leading tone: one half step below A. When V resolves to i, that G♯ pulls upward to A, creating the same strong V-i authentic cadence that major keys use. This is why harmonic minor was developed. The exotic augmented 2nd and the III+ chord are the price paid for that resolution strength.
📝 THINK FIRST · THEN CHECK
In your notebook: list the three triads in harmonic minor that differ from natural minor, and explain why each one changed. Write your answer before revealing the model response.

Three triads change when you raise scale degree 7 by a half step: (1) III becomes III+ because the raised 7th is the fifth of the III chord, stretching it from a perfect fifth to an augmented fifth. (2) v becomes V (major) because the raised 7th is the third of the V chord, changing it from a minor third to a major third. (3) VII (major) becomes vii° (diminished) because the chord that was rooted on the natural scale degree 7 now sits on the raised degree 7, making a diminished triad. All three changes trace back to that single raised note.

💪 THE ONE THING TO REMEMBER

Harmonic minor = natural minor with scale degree 7 raised by one half step. That one change buys a strong V-i cadence (the whole point) but costs an augmented 2nd (the exotic gap between degrees 6 and 7) and produces an augmented triad on III (the III+ chord). The diatonic triads are i, ii°, III+, iv, V (major!), VI, vii°. Keep the V in your ear: that is what harmonic minor was invented for.
