LESSON 4.01
Melodic Minor and Three-Form Comparison
Lesson
📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
Why does a minor scale change its notes depending on which direction it goes?
You already know natural and harmonic minor. The third form, melodic minor, solves a specific problem that arises when voices sing upward. Today you put all three forms side by side and understand exactly what each one does and why.

2

3

🎵 PART 1 · THE PROBLEM WITH HARMONIC MINOR

Harmonic minor raises scale degree 7 to create a strong pull toward the tonic. That is great for chords. But for a melody moving upward through scale degrees 6 and 7, that raised 7 creates an augmented second (three half steps) between degrees 6 and 7, an awkward leap for a singing voice. Melodic minor solves this by also raising scale degree 6 when going up.

The fix: raise scale degree 6 as well, so the leap becomes a smooth whole step. That is the ascending melodic minor.

Purple chips = raised degrees. Descending melodic minor reverts completely to natural minor.
🎵 JAZZ NOTE
In jazz, the ascending form (raised 6 and 7) is used in both directions. Composers such as Miles Davis and John Coltrane use this "jazz melodic minor" or "acoustic scale" freely without switching back on the way down. The classical descending revert is mainly a Baroque and Classical-era convention.
🎹 PART 3 · WHY TWO DIRECTIONS?

Going up toward the tonic, a melody needs momentum, so the raised 7 (and raised 6 to avoid the awkward leap) works with that pull. Going down away from the tonic, there is no upward pull to create, so the natural minor version is smooth and relaxed. The scale literally changes its notes to match the direction of travel.

📚 PART 4 · KEY VOCABULARY
⚡ TAP EACH CARD to reveal the definition:
Melodic Minor
(tap to reveal)
A form of minor that raises scale degrees 6 and 7 when ascending to eliminate the awkward augmented second created by harmonic minor. Descending, it reverts to natural minor.
Augmented Second
(tap to reveal)
An interval of three half steps. Found between the natural 6th and raised 7th of harmonic minor. Sounds and feels awkward in a stepwise melody; melodic minor eliminates it by raising the 6th as well.
Ascending Melodic Minor
(tap to reveal)
Raise both scale degrees 6 and 7. In A minor: A-B-C-D-E-F♯-G♯-A. Two whole steps below the tonic; sounds almost major in that region.
Descending Melodic Minor
(tap to reveal)
Reverts to natural minor (no raised degrees). In A minor: A-G-F-E-D-C-B-A. No upward pull is needed when moving down, so the raised degrees are unnecessary.

🤖 WORTH KNOWING · TRANSCRIPTION APPS
AI transcription apps can often detect melodic minor from an audio recording and label it correctly. What they cannot do is decide, inside your own composition or analysis, whether the ascending or descending form better serves a particular musical moment. That choice is a musical judgment call, not a lookup.

💪 THE ONE THING TO REMEMBER

Melodic minor raises both degrees 6 and 7 going up to create a smooth two-whole-step approach to the tonic. Going down, the urgency disappears, so it reverts to natural minor. That one direction-sensitive rule separates melodic minor from every other form.
