🎵 Today’s Focus

Modes are not just scales – they have signature chord moves. This lesson is about modal schemas: the short, repeating chord loops that make Mixolydian rock, Dorian minor sound bright, and Aeolian feel like a pure natural-minor flow. You will learn to hear and build three of them.

By the End of This Lesson…

    Identify the Mixolydian and Dorian schemas in pop and folk music
  • 2
    Build characteristic chord progressions in Dorian and Mixolydian
  • 3
    Explain why modal harmony sounds different from major/minor tonal harmony

  • Your notebook or staff paper

Key Vocabulary

Before diving into the schemas themselves, make sure these terms are clear. Add them to your vocabulary section in your notebook.

Mixolydian Schema
A chord loop built around the ♭VII chord – the subtonic triad. Example: I–♭VII–IV–I. Common in rock and folk. (OMT p. 628)
Dorian Schema
A minor-mode loop with a major IV chord. The raised 6th of Dorian makes IV major instead of minor. Example: i–IV. (OMT p. 627)
Aeolian Schema
A natural-minor loop using only unaltered scale degrees. Examples: III–VII–i and i–VII–VI–VII. (OMT p. 626)
Modal Tonic
A tonal center established by repetition, not by V–I tension. Modal music has no leading tone pulling up to the tonic.

Part 1 – Mixolydian Schemas

Mixolydian is like a major scale with one difference: scale degree 7 is lowered by a half step. That single change removes the leading tone (the note that pulls urgently up to the tonic in major keys) and creates a characteristic chord: ♭VII, the subtonic triad.

The rule: In Mixolydian, ♭VII takes the job that V usually has in major. Because scale degree 7 is flat, there is no leading-tone pull. The result is a relaxed, rocking quality instead of a tense dominant resolution.

Three common Mixolydian schemas appear repeatedly in pop and folk:

  • Pendulum: I – ♭VII – I
  • Three-chord loop: I – IV – ♭VII – I
  • Classic Mixolydian loop: I – ♭VII – IV – I

In each case, the ♭VII chord is the key player. The loop never reaches V–I; it rocks between I and ♭VII instead. That avoidance of a dominant cadence is what marks these progressions as modal, not tonal.

Visual 1 – Mixolydian Schema: I – ♭VII – IV – I

The ♭VII box (gold border) is the characteristic Mixolydian chord. No V means no leading-tone tension – the loop rocks freely.

Many rock and folk songs use this loop for verses or as an entire song structure. The pendulum version (I – ♭VII – I) strips it down even further – just two chords alternating, with no harmonic goal except the return to I.

🎵 Listening Tip

Search YouTube for "Mixolydian mode rock examples" or try classic songs like "Sweet Home Alabama" or "Norwegian Wood" – notice how the harmony never drives toward a strong V-I cadence. The ♭VII chord gives it that characteristic lift-and-return.

Part 2 – Dorian Schemas

Dorian is a minor mode – but it has one note that sets it apart from natural minor (Aeolian): scale degree 6 is raised by a half step. That single raised note changes everything about the IV chord.

The rule: In Aeolian, the chord built on scale degree 4 is minor (iv). In Dorian, because scale degree 6 is raised, that same chord becomes major (IV). The raised 6th is the 3rd of the IV chord – and what makes it major is exactly what identifies the mode as Dorian.

The core Dorian schemas are:

  • Core: i – IV
  • Extended: i – ♭VII – IV
Visual 2 – Dorian Schema: i – IV

The IV chord (green border) is major in Dorian. That’s the fingerprint: minor tonic + major IV. In Aeolian, IV would be minor (iv).

The major IV chord is the Dorian fingerprint. If you hear a progression that feels minor but has a bright, lifted quality on the IV chord, Dorian is likely the reason. The raised 6th that makes IV major also adds a subtle warmth to the mode – it sounds darker than major but less heavy than natural minor.

In practice (D Dorian example):
i = D minor (D – F – A)
IV = G major (G – B♮ – D) – the B♮ is the raised 6th of D Dorian
Compare: In D Aeolian, that chord would be G minor (G – B♭ – D)
🎵 Listening Tip

Search YouTube for "Dorian mode song example" or try "Oye Como Va" by Santana – the minor-but-bright feeling comes from exactly this i–IV move.

Part 3 – Aeolian Schemas and Key vs. Mode

Aeolian is natural minor – no alterations from the key signature. Its schemas use only the chords built naturally on the scale, with no raised degrees:

  • Descending schema: III – VII – i
  • Four-chord loop: i – VII – VI – VII

Notice that VII here is a major chord built on the flat 7th – the same ♭VII that appears in Mixolydian. That makes sense: both modes have a lowered 7th. What differs is the tonic: Aeolian has a minor tonic (i), Mixolydian has a major tonic (I).

Visual 3 – Aeolian Schemas

Aeolian uses only natural minor chords – no raised degrees. The yellow box captures the principle of all three modal schemas: tonic stability through repetition, not tension.

Key vs. Mode – the essential distinction:
A key is tonal: it has a hierarchy with V pulling to I, a leading tone, and clear function labels (tonic, dominant, pre-dominant). A mode is not tonal in this sense: there is no leading tone in Mixolydian, Dorian, or Aeolian, and no V–I cadence driving toward home. Instead, the tonic feels stable because the loop keeps returning to it. Repetition replaces tension.

This means you can have a piece "in D Dorian" that never once uses a D major chord – the tonic D minor just keeps appearing, anchored by the characteristic Dorian schema (i–IV). The raised 6th in the melody and the major IV chord in the harmony are what tell you: this is Dorian, not D natural minor.

Think it through: What would tell you a piece is in D Dorian rather than D natural minor (D Aeolian)?
Write your answer in your notebook before reading on. Look for the IV chord – is it G minor (Aeolian) or G major (Dorian)? That difference is the Dorian identifier.

🤖 WORTH KNOWING · MODAL LOOPS AND AI
A loop generator or AI music tool can produce a convincing Dorian or Mixolydian vamp in seconds. What it will not explain is why that loop sounds different from an ordinary major or minor progression, the raised sixth that makes a Dorian IV chord major, or the missing leading tone that gives a Mixolydian loop its unresolved, rocking character. Hearing and explaining that difference is the actual point of this lesson.

The One Thing to Remember

Modal harmony gets its stability from repetition, not from V – I tension. The characteristic chord of each mode (♭VII for Mixolydian, major IV for Dorian) is the loop’s anchor – not a dominant waiting to resolve.
