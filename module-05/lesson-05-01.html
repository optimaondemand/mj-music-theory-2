LESSON 5.01
Minor Key Signatures and the Circle of Fifths

📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
If two keys share the same key signature, how do you tell which one the music is actually in?
Minor keys borrow their key signatures from major keys. That means a single key signature can belong to two different keys. Today you learn the relative major/minor relationship, read the inner ring of the Circle of Fifths, and develop the ear-and-eye strategies for deciding major or minor.

🎵 PART 1 · THE RELATIVE MAJOR/MINOR PAIR

Every major key has a relative minor that shares its exact key signature. The relative minor's tonic is scale degree 6 of the major scale, which is the same as going down three half steps from the major tonic. Flip the relationship: from the minor tonic, go up three half steps to find the relative major.

A is scale degree 6 of C major. Both share zero sharps and zero flats.

Quick rule for any major key: count up to scale degree 6 (or down 3 half steps) to reach the relative minor tonic. The key signature stays identical.

🎶 PART 2 · THE CIRCLE OF FIFTHS WITH THE MINOR RING

The Circle of Fifths has two rings: the outer ring (major keys in green) and the inner ring (their relative minor keys in purple). Each minor key sits directly inside its relative major at the same clock position, sharing the same key signature. Moving clockwise adds sharps; moving counter-clockwise adds flats.

Green = major key (outer ring). Purple = relative minor (inner ring). Each pair shares the same key signature.
💡 SHARP SIDE PAIRS (0 to 4 sharps)
C major / Am (0)  |  G major / Em (1♯)  |  D major / Bm (2♯)  |  A major / F♯m (3♯)  |  E major / C♯m (4♯)
💡 FLAT SIDE PAIRS (1 to 4 flats)
F major / Dm (1♭)  |  B♭ major / Gm (2♭)  |  E♭ major / Cm (3♭)  |  A♭ major / Fm (4♭)
🎯 PART 3 · MAJOR OR MINOR? HOW TO DECIDE

When you see a key signature, you have two candidates (major and its relative minor). These four questions help you decide which one applies:

MAJOR OR MINOR? DECISION CARD
1
Which pitch sounds like home?
Listen for the pitch the music keeps returning to or ending on. That is the tonic. If it matches the key signature's major key, you are in major; if it matches the relative minor, you are in minor.
2
What is the final cadence chord?
The last chord almost always resolves to the tonic. A major chord at the end suggests major; a minor chord suggests minor.
3
Is the tonic chord major or minor?
In a two-sharp key signature (D major / B minor), the chord built on D is major, the chord built on B is minor. Which one does the piece use most as its home chord?
4
Raised-7 accidental? Strong clue for minor.
In harmonic minor the raised scale degree 7 appears throughout the score as an accidental. If you keep seeing a ♯ that is not in the key signature on the pitch that is one step below the home pitch, the piece is almost certainly in minor.

💪 THE ONE THING TO REMEMBER

A minor key's signature is borrowed from its relative major. To find the relative minor, go down three half steps from the major tonic (or up to scale degree 6). When a key signature could be either, listen for the home pitch, check the final chord, and watch for the raised-7 accidental as a minor clue.
