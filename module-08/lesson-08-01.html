LESSON 8.01
Borrowed Harmony: Tonicization in Minor Keys

🎵 TODAY'S FOCUS
How can a piece in A minor suddenly feel like it is in C major for a moment -- and then return home?
You already know how secondary dominants tonicize chords in major keys. The same tool works in minor -- you just need to know which chords are the common targets. Today you will build and resolve secondary dominants to III, VI, and VII in a minor key, and you will learn to spot them by their chromatic note.

2
I will be able to resolve each secondary dominant correctly to its target chord.
3
I will be able to identify a tonicization in a score by locating the chromatic (applied leading-tone) note.

🎵 PART 1 · THE CONCEPT: SECONDARY DOMINANTS IN MINOR

A secondary dominant is a dominant 7th chord built to target a chord other than the home tonic. You already know this in major keys. In minor keys, the concept is identical, but the most common targets shift. In a minor key, the three most common chords to tonicize are:

III
The relative major
Most common tonicization in minor
VI
The submediant major
Creates a warm harmonic turn
VII
The subtonic major
Common in Baroque and Classical
💡 THE KEY TO SPOTTING THEM
Most secondary dominants contain a chromatic note -- a pitch that does not belong in the home key. That chromatic note is the applied leading tone: it rises by half step to the root of the target chord. Find the chromatic note, and you have usually found the tonicization -- except for V7/III, a special case you will meet next.
🎹 PART 2 · V7/III IN A MINOR -- THE MOST IMPORTANT CASE

In A minor, the most common secondary dominant is V7/III. The target chord is C major (III -- the relative major). C major's own dominant is G, so the secondary dominant is G7 (G–B–D–F). Here is the surprise: every one of those notes -- G, B, D, F -- already belongs to the A natural minor scale. No accidental is needed at all.

V7/III in A minor: G7 resolving to C major (III)
B is the leading tone into C, but unlike the other two cases in this module, B already belongs to A natural minor -- G7 needs zero accidentals. That is because the diatonic VII chord in natural minor (built on G) is already a major triad, so it already functions as the applied dominant of III. The 7th of G7 (the note F) falls by step to E.
🎹 PART 3 · THE THREE COMMON SECONDARY DOMINANTS IN A MINOR

Here are the three most-used secondary dominants in A minor. In each case, the chromatic note is the clue that identifies the tonicization:

Label Chord (in A minor) Chord tones Chromatic clue Resolves to
V7/III G7 G – B – D – F None -- already diatonic (B resolves to C) C major (III)
V7/VI C7 C – E – G – B♭ B♭ (applied leading tone to F) F major (VI)
V7/VII D7 D – F♯ – A – C F♯ (applied leading tone to G) G major (VII)
💡 SPOT THE TONICIZATION: THREE-STEP CUE CARD
1
Find the chromatic note -- the pitch that does not belong in the home key's natural minor scale.
2
That chromatic note is the applied leading tone. It rises by half step -- that half step lands on the root of the target chord.
3
Name the secondary dominant as V7/[target chord Roman numeral] in the home key.
✍️ PART 4 · PRACTICE: IDENTIFY THE TONICIZATION

Use the table and cue card above. Work out your answer first, then tap.

🤖 WORTH KNOWING · CHORD-RECOGNITION APPS
AI chord-recognition apps can label a secondary dominant in real time as you listen to a recording, and they sometimes mislabel ambiguous chromatic passages, especially cases like V7/III where the chord is fully diatonic. The three-step method this lesson teaches, finding the chromatic note and tracing its resolution, is exactly what lets you catch and correct an error like that yourself.

💪 THE ONE THING TO REMEMBER

The chromatic note is always the clue. Find the pitch that does not belong in the home key's natural minor scale -- that is the applied leading tone. Follow it upward by half step, and you land on the root of the tonicized chord.
