🎵 PART 1 · MAJOR SCALES AND KEY SIGNATURES

A major scale is a sequence of eight notes following the pattern whole-whole-half-whole-whole-whole-half (W-W-H-W-W-W-H). Half steps fall between scale degrees 3-4 and 7-8. Every major key has a unique key signature that places the accidentals for you.

C major (no sharps or flats). Red = half steps. W = whole step, H = half step. Half steps always fall at 3-4 and 7-8.
💡 KEY SIGNATURES TO KNOW
Sharp keys: G (1♯), D (2♯), A (3♯), E (4♯), B (5♯). Flat keys: F (1♭), B♭ (2♭), E♭ (3♭), A♭ (4♭). Order of sharps: F♯-C♯-G♯-D♯-A♯-E♯-B♯. Order of flats reverses: B♭-E♭-A♭-D♭-G♭-C♭-F♭. Theory II expects you to write these from memory - use this module to verify your speed.
🎵 PART 2 · TRIAD QUALITIES

A triad is a stack of three pitches built from thirds. The combination of those two thirds determines the quality. There are exactly four qualities you need to recognize by sight and by sound:

FOUR TRIAD QUALITIES · INTERVAL STACKS ON THE TREBLE STAFF
Each triad sits at its own real pitch on the treble staff, root to fifth. Notice the augmented triad needs an explicit sharp sign on its top note (G♯) – that raised fifth is what makes it sound stretched. Read the interval stack from root up: major = M3 then m3. Minor = m3 then M3. Diminished = m3 twice. Augmented = M3 twice.
💡 ROMAN NUMERAL NOTATION REVIEW
Uppercase Roman numerals (I, II, IV) = major quality. Lowercase (ii, iii, vi) = minor. Lowercase + circle (vii°) = diminished. Uppercase + plus sign (III+) = augmented. In major keys, the diatonic triads are I, ii, iii, IV, V, vi, vii°.
🎹 PART 3 · CADENCE TYPES

A cadence is a chord progression that signals a pause or ending. Theory II analysis uses cadences constantly. Here are the five types you must know cold:

CADENCE REFERENCE CARD
PAC
Perfect Authentic Cadence
V (or V7) → I, both chords in root position, tonic note in the soprano. The strongest cadence - a full stop.
V → I
IAC
Imperfect Authentic Cadence
V → I but with at least one exception: inverted chord, non-tonic soprano, or V replaced by vii°. Sounds like a PAC but slightly open.
V → I*
HC
Half Cadence
Any chord → V. The phrase ends on dominant - sounds like a comma, not a period. Pulls forward to the next phrase.
? → V
Plagal
Plagal Cadence
IV → I. The "Amen" cadence. Softer and more final-sounding than the authentic cadence. Common in hymns and gospel.
IV → I
Deceptive
Deceptive Cadence
V → vi. The ear expects V → I, but the music surprises with vi instead. Used to extend a phrase or delay resolution.
V → vi
💡 PHRASE MODEL REMINDER
Harmonic phrases in common-practice music follow the Tonic - Pre-Dominant - Dominant - Tonic model (T-PD-D-T). A PAC closes the T-PD-D-T arch. A HC pauses on D and opens a new arch. Theory II will use this model constantly, so make sure it is automatic.
